Project Report task-1-presentation: Digital Literacy Infographic
For this task, I designed an infographic using Canva, a free design tool. My design, titled "Digital Literacy Unlocked," covers three essential digital age topics.
First, it defines digital literacy as the ability to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information responsibly, noting it is much more than just using a smartphone. Second, the visual outlines safe internet practices. It advises users to lock down accounts with strong passwords and Two-Factor Authentication (2FA). It also warns users to spot phishing scams by avoiding suspicious links and reminds viewers to be mindful of their digital footprint, since what goes online stays online. Finally, it details proper email etiquette. Tips include keeping subject lines clear, using professional greetings and sign-offs, getting straight to the point, and proofreading before sending.
One thing I found challenging was condensing these critical concepts into short, impactful bullet points. Balancing visual elements with necessary text without making the slide look cluttered was a highly interesting design exercise.

Project Report task-3-platforms: Google Workspace Collaboration
For this task, I utilized Google Workspace—specifically Google Forms and Google Sheets—to build a "Digital Literacy Awareness Quiz." I created a five-question survey designed to test my batchmates' knowledge on essential digital topics, including password strength, professional email etiquette, social media footprint permanence, and Two-Factor Authentication. The form incorporated a mix of multiple-choice, true/false, and short-answer formats, allowing me to practice gathering various types of qualitative and quantitative data. I then linked this form to a Google Sheet to automatically capture, organize, and view the incoming responses in a structured database format.
Mastering these tools will be incredibly beneficial throughout my academic journey over the next four years. Project Report task-4-email-etiquette: The Impact of Poor Digital Communication
A classic example of poor digital communication causing a real-world problem is the scenario of a college student losing a prestigious internship offer due to an inappropriate tweet. Frustrated after a difficult exam, a student posted a highly profane and disparaging remark about their university and a specific professor. Because their public profile was linked to their real name, the hiring company’s HR department easily found the tweet during a routine background check and promptly revoked the internship offer, citing a violation of their professional core values.
This devastating consequence could have been easily avoided. The student failed to practice the "think before you post" rule. Instead of venting publicly on a permanent digital platform, they should have processed their frustration offline, discussed it with a friend privately, or at the very least, ensured their account was strictly private and disconnected from their professional identity.

Project Report task-5-cybercrime: Cybercrime Awareness and Personal Habit Changes
While researching cybercrime prevention for this project, I was most surprised by the sheer sophistication of modern financial frauds targeting college students, particularly the "Telegram task" and fake internship scams. I previously assumed that mostly older, less tech-savvy individuals fell for online fraud. However, the psychological manipulation, the illusion of easy money, and the professional appearance of these fake recruiters make them highly deceptive, even for digital natives. Furthermore, learning how easily a screen-sharing app like AnyDesk can be weaponized by fake "customer support" to steal OTPs was eye-opening. As a direct result of this research, the one habit I will personally change is my approach to public Wi-Fi. I will stop checking my bank balance or making UPI payments while connected to the open campus network, and instead rely strictly on my private cellular data for any sensitive financial transactions.
